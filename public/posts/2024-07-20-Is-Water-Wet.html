I've had lengthy debates on this topic for years now. It seems that every couple of years this question will be posed in one place or another and will once again spark conversation and great division among people who are otherwise in complete agreement. I myself have had great fun attempting to argue my stance on the argument to those of varying degrees of debate prowess.

However, being the overanalyzer that I am, I have noticed similar threads in these debates every time that I have them that lead me to believe that the issue is even more nuanced than I initially believed.

While much of the time has been spent in a direct conflictual debate, one of person vs. person, opinion vs. opinion, I tend to find myself leaning towards dialogue.

Dialogue(or discussion) rather than debate(or argument) is much more effective at producing solutions and understanding. When one or both parties are focused on convincing the other side that they are wrong, very little headway can be made in the direction of finding the actual truth. This is because typically both parties are singularly focused on 'winning' rather than understanding.

In my observations, it is much more common for parties to have a lack of understanding rather than an outright disagreement on a topic. Don't get me wrong, there are plenty of outright disagreements where people understand the other parties' position and opinions explicitly and disagree, but I have found that it is far more common to see ineffective communication leading to disconnects of understanding between the parties, which leads to further arguments and debates.

It was with this mindset that I reevaluated the liquid conundrum and attempted to parse the past debates I have had, with the intention of understanding those whom I originally was trying to convince.

I found that the majority of the misunderstandings led back to a singular disconnect. That of definition. The entire debate hinges on semantics, and the definition of the adjective "wet" covers most semantic qualities of the word. This was not a novel concept to me, as during many of the arguments I based my stance on the "objective" truth of the dictionary definition.

The breakthrough was found when taking into consideration the relationship a dictionary has with the language it represents. When considered from a broad point of view, many consider a dictionary to be the ultimate authority on a language, one that solidly defines the meanings of words and phrases to police the communication that takes place using the language. However, this could not be further from the truth.

A dictionary is not, and will never be, the final authority on language as long as there exists a population actively communicating with the language. Rather, a dictionary exists to document and reflect the population of active users of a language. If our dictionaries were written during the earlier times of the English language, many words and phrases would have wildly different definitions based on how the language was used at the time.

This means that true, objectively correct semantic definitions for problems such as this do not exist, as the personal cultural usage of a word varies greatly from individual to individual, from community to community. This means that when it comes to issues that hinge entirely on semantical definitions, such as "is water wet," disagreeing parties can both be objectively right in their stance on the issue.

While this revelation does not please the part of me that yearns for objective facts, it helps greatly in my further pursuit of achieving a greater understanding of those I find myself in debate with. I believe that many issues can be resolved with effective communication, and understanding these fundamental ideas that can cause such major disconnects should enable me to more effectively communicate and understand others in the future.
